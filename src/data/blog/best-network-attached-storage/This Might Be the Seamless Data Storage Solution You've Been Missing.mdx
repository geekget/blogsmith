## Who this is for

A network-attached storage device, or NAS, is a small always-on computer generally used for backing up computers and serving files to devices on your local network. It includes at least one, but usually two or more, hard-drive bays, a Linux-based operating system optimized for network storage, and enough CPU power and RAM to do everything it needs to do while using far less power than a repurposed old computer.

While some NAS models come with built-in storage, most do not and are sold diskless. You should expect to purchase additional hard drives, but it also follows that you can tailor your storage to meet your needs and budget.

Unlike a portable SSD or an external hard drive, a NAS with two or more hard drives can provide data redundancy for protection against single or multiple drive failure, copying the contents of one drive over to the other automatically.

A NAS is great if you have a large media library, because you can store your files in one place and stream them locally to computers, phones, tablets, speakers, or media centers throughout your house (or even outside it). A NAS is a useful tool for people who need to store thousands of raw photo files, terabytes of raw video, gigabytes of digital music, or backups of their movie and TV collection.

You should consider a NAS if you have more than one computer at home, since you can back them all up to the NAS rather than connect an external backup drive to each computer. And if you want to protect your data and backups from theft and natural disasters, a good NAS is capable of uploading files directly to a [cloud backup service](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-online-backup-service/), too.

A NAS is useful if you have too much data to store in Dropbox or Google Drive, or if you don’t trust your data to cloud storage providers.

Most NAS devices can also act as email, database, and virtual private network (VPN) servers. You can use them for BitTorrent, to host [CMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Content_management_system), [CRM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer_relationship_management), and e-commerce software, or as DVRs for [networked security cameras](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/home-garden/safety-home-garden/). A NAS is a computer, so it can do almost anything a Linux computer can do.

## How we picked

Since this guide’s original publication in 2011, we’ve surveyed many two- and four-bay NAS from reputable vendors. That includes models from Asustor, Buffalo, Netgear, QNAP, Seagate, Synology, Thecus, Ugreen, WD, and Zyxel. We’ve read professional NAS reviews on [CNET](http://www.cnet.com/), [PCMag](https://www.pcmag.com/), [PCWorld](http://www.pcworld.com/), [SmallNetBuilder](http://www.smallnetbuilder.com/), and [StorageReview.com](http://www.storagereview.com/), and we’ve also looked at customer reviews on Amazon and Newegg.

When deciding which models to test, we paid attention to key features that most home NAS buyers need:

- **Two drive bays:** For most home uses, a two-drive NAS protects your data by mirroring the contents of one drive to the other (RAID 1). Single-drive NAS devices provide less data protection, while NAS boxes with more bays offer more complex RAID configurations.
- **Price:** For home use, we’re searching around $250 to $500 for a two-bay NAS (sans hard drives). More than that, and you’re entering business-class territory. You sacrifice speed and utility on a cheaper model.
- **CPU and RAM:** We prefer a multicore Intel Celeron or AMD Ryzen processor. We looked for 2 GB of RAM at minimum.
- **Easy-to-use software:** The best examples work like Windows or MacOS, so they should be familiar to computer users. The worst are clunky, like the configuration screens of early 2000s routers.
- **Wide support for backups:** A NAS should support computer backups on Windows, Macs, and Android or iPhones. It should also allow you to back up easily to a [cloud storage service](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-online-backup-service/) like Backblaze or iDrive.
- **Media-streaming capabilities:** NAS boxes can stream videos and music to various devices in your home. You can do this using software from the manufacturer or third-party programs like Plex.
- **At least two USB ports:** Between backups, dongles, and other accessories, it’s nice to have at least two USB ports on a NAS.
- **Third-party application support:** If you want to add features or services to your NAS, you need to do that with third-party software — including media center software, web-server software, analytics, and more.
- **Warranty and support:** The models we tested come with two- or three-year warranties. They also offer tech support via phone, email, online knowledge bases, and forums. Tutorials and videos are accessible on their websites.

## How we tested

We set up each NAS while looking at the web interface’s organization and features. We tested ease of use by configuring user and group accounts, as well as file and folder access permissions. We checked to see if the NAS offered secure remote access via Android and iOS mobile apps or the web. We inspected each NAS for drive trays or slots that make the drives easy to remove while still fitting tightly and securely.

The easiest way to measure real-world NAS performance, at least for how a home NAS is typically used, is to copy files to and from the NAS and calculate the data rate. Since 2015, we’ve run read-and-write tests the simplest way we can: by copying files over Ethernet and measuring the elapsed time. This round, as none of our picks came with pre-installed storage, we installed two or four 3.5-inch hard drives in each NAS as needed, connected each model via 2.5-gigabit Ethernet to a router with 2.5-gigabit Ethernet ports, and connected a PC with a 2.5-gigabit USB-to-Ethernet adapter to the router. We used Windows’ built-in Robocopy file-copying tool to read and write two folders to each NAS: a 32 GB music folder with 6,154 MP3 files, as well as a folder with an 8.1 GB MKV video file and a 7.07 GB Linux ISO file.

### Flaws but not dealbreakers

**Synology has only certified its branded drives as “compatible.”** Prior to October 2025, Synology had deemed third-party drives like WD Red NAS hard drives as “incompatible.” However, after that date, the latest and future versions of Synology’s operating system (DSM 7.3) supports third-party drives, including those from Seagate and Western Digital. To that end, we tested the DS225+ and DS425+ with both Synology-certified drives and WD Red Plus NAS drives, with identical setup experiences and similar test results.

**The drive bays aren’t lockable.** The DS225+ has simple slide-in drive trays, which can’t be locked for security. More expensive options from Synology, like the DS425+, DS725+, and DS925+, have more robust drive bays with a key lock. These locks aren’t high security by any means, but they should keep wandering fingers from popping a spinning drive out inadvertently, and we wish the DS225+ included this option.

**It has relatively limited connections**. The DS225+ has only one 2.5 GbE port, and its second Ethernet port handles only slower, 1-gigabit speeds. This NAS also lacks an HDMI port, so you can’t connect it to a TV as a web-browsing computer or use it as a video set-top box.

**The DS225+’s memory upgrades are limited.** It comes with 2 GB of memory, which you can triple to 6 GB. Folks who want to upgrade beyond that for their business needs should look at the Synology DS725+ or DS925+, both of which can be upgraded to 32 GB.

**Virtual machines are supported but only to a point.** Some technical-minded folks will want to offload functions from their primary PC to a side server like the DS225+. The NAS supports up to two [virtual machines](https://www.synology.com/en-global/dsm/feature/virtual_machine_manager) (VM) running simultaneously. These VMs act like independent Linux or Windows PCs running on the NAS, which can be dedicated to functions like downloading and transcoding 4K video files, or running a personal VPN, a security system (firewall), or a test web server for your projects. Folks who want more capability beyond file sharing and a few server-side apps for their business or homelab should look at the Synology DS725+ or DS925+.

## What to look forward to

Faster Ethernet ports are becoming commonplace on routers that support speeds of 2.5, 5, or 10 gigabits per second. This is a significant improvement over the 1 gigabit Ethernet ports common to older routers and servers. When you decide to update your network to support faster network speeds, make sure to check your router for 2.5 GbE or 10 GbE LAN (local network) ports.

We’ve recently looked at SSD-only NAS boxes like the [Asustor Flashstor 6](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/67473/211412/4/206140/?merchant=Amazon) and [QNAP TBS-464-8G](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/67474/211413/4/206141/?merchant=Amazon), and considered the ability to add M.2 NVMe SSDs to the tested [Synology DiskStation DS725+](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/79263/234887/4/247226/?merchant=Amazon) and [DiskStation DS925+](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/79264/234888/4/247227/?merchant=Amazon). However, SSD-only NAS (and the [internal SSDs](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-ssds/) you’d have to buy to populate them) are too expensive for most people. Each internal 4 TB SSD is approaching $700 as we write this guide, and for the same amount of money you can purchase 32 TB [Seagate Iron Wolf Pro](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/79265/234889/4/247228/?merchant=Amazon) hard drives for eight times the storage, or save a wallet full of money buying a more reasonably sized set of hard drives. Installing SSDs in a NAS instead of spinning hard drives will be quieter and potentially faster with multi-gig Ethernet connections, but we’re holding off testing and recommending these solutions until prices on SSDs become more economical.

## The competition

Both Synology and QNAP sell many, many NAS devices, all of which run the same software but vary in processor type and speed, RAM, number of drive bays, and features. Our picks offer the best combination of performance, features, and price for most home uses, and Synology’s and QNAP’s respective software offerings are more powerful and polished than those of other NAS makers.

The [QNAP TS-264-8G](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/58680/196099/4/173848/?merchant=Walmart) and [QNAP TS-464](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/58700/196084/4/173869/?merchant=Amazon) were our former top pick and upgrade pick, respectively. Both were outperformed by our new picks on file transfers, particularly when saving large files and small music files to the respective NAS’s storage. Both are still recommended if you need a NAS with HDMI output for a quasi-PC experience or if you want to use the NAS as a media server playing back video on a TV.

We tested the [Ugreen NASync DH2300](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/79266/234890/4/247229/?merchant=Amazon) and compared it with our budget two-bay NAS pick and the Synology BeeStations. While the Ugreen NAS is cheaper than our budget pick by more than $50, the Ugreen device requires an included screwdriver to install hard drives, while the QNAP is tool-less and quite a bit easier to install. The Ugreen NAS is also limited to a single 1-gigabit Ethernet port, while the QNAP TS-216G has a 2.5 GbE port, and subsequently the QNAP tested measurably faster than the Ugreen DH2300 on our file transfer tests.

We tested the [QNAP HS-264](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/58682/196059/4/173851/?merchant=Amazon) because its slim horizontal body, dual HDMI ports, and fanless operation looked attractive. It will fit well in a studio apartment or in your living room. While it was fast across the board, the top surface of the NAS became hot during operation. It also required a screwdriver to install hard drives, so it’s not as quick to set up as our pick. Consider it if you’re a cinephile who wants to watch movies directly from the NAS and if you need a media server to store your 1080p and 4K video file collection.
