## Who mesh-networking systems are for

Before you toss everything out and get a mesh system, you should try [moving your router](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/blog/make-wi-fi-suck-less-working-from-home/) to a central location—in smaller houses a single router can actually be [more effective](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/blog/wi-fi-router-good-as-mesh/) than mesh networking.

But if you have a house that a [single powerful router](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-wi-fi-router/) can’t cover well, you probably need a mesh system. A home larger than 2,300 square feet (depending on the layout), a large apartment or small house with signal-killing interior walls made of lath-and-plaster, brick, stucco, or concrete blocks, or a tall, narrow building like a three-story townhouse could all benefit from a mesh setup with multiple nodes.

A mesh network is composed of two or more parts. A mesh router or base unit is the device you set up first. It connects to your home’s internet (via an Ethernet connection to a cable modem or the gateway router) and broadcasts Wi-Fi. A mesh node or satellite is the device that connects back to your router (or another node) to extend that network and provide a more reliable Wi-Fi connection over a greater area. Most systems come with one or two of them. Sometimes they are physically identical to the base unit and sometimes they aren’t.

If you already have a good router that you like and need just a little more range in part of your house, you might consider adding a wireless extender. (Here’s our comprehensive [guide to wireless extenders](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-wi-fi-extender/).)

Another option is a mesh extender, which, like a mesh-networking system, automatically hands your connection off from router to extender and back, using the same network name; that makes the mesh experience a little more seamless. Mesh extenders may improve coverage in dead spots if you already have a decent wireless router, though they showed mixed results in our [extender guide testing](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-wi-fi-extender/) compared with full-blown mesh networking systems.

### A wired network is always faster than a wireless one

If your house is wired for Ethernet, you don’t need a mesh-networking system. Mesh shines when you don’t have wires, don’t want wires, and have lots of trouble spots (or one really big trouble spot) with poor or no coverage.

A mesh system won’t necessarily make your internet faster at short to medium range. As shown in the [performance testing of standalone routers](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-wi-fi-router/#how-we-tested-plus-the-results), the best Wi-Fi mesh systems did just as well as our upgrade standalone router pick, the [TP-Link Archer BE550](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/59798/197969/4/193682/?merchant=Amazon). Mesh can offer better coverage and lower latency in a wider area, which makes your connection feel faster throughout the house because your devices aren’t grabbing at faint wisps of signal.

A good standalone Wi-Fi router can handle multiple devices, as long as those devices all have good connections. Even one device with a poor connection can bring the quality of a single router’s network down, eating up all of the available airtime, starving the rest. The best mesh networks ensure good connections among devices, the base unit, and any satellites, reducing the likelihood of a poorly connected device slowing down the others.

## How we picked the best mesh network

Over the past five years, we’ve tested Wi-Fi mesh-networking systems from almost 20 manufacturers (AmpliFi, Arris, Asus, D-Link, Eero, Google, Gryphon, Motorola, Linksys, Netgear, Synology, TP-Link, Trendnet, Ubiquiti, Vilo, Wyze, and Zyxel) in order to find the best Wi-Fi mesh network system.

To reproduce the real-world activity of a busy home network, we used laptops simulating everyday tasks, spaced around 3,000 square feet of a three-and-a-half-story suburban home.

We tested for top speeds (streaming simulated 4K video and file downloads), good coverage in spots around the house, and responsiveness (simulating three simultaneous browsing sessions on a busy network).

Here are our key criteria:

- **Ease of setup and administration:** You should be able to get your home on the internet in less than half an hour with a mesh network.
- **Good speed test results:** In our tests, network speed—or throughput—varies from “This YouTube video will never finish loading” to “You can download a video game in an instant.”
- **Good range test results:** You should be able to connect to a well-placed mesh system from anywhere in your house. We tested each system to see its maximum potential when close to the base unit, as well as in trouble spots in the home through walls and Wi-Fi blocking objects like refrigerators.
- **Quick, responsive network results:** Slow internet sucks. Latency—or lag—is the time spent waiting for the next thing to happen. A great mesh system minimizes that wait even if the network is busy.
- **Expandability:** You should be able to add more nodes later to extend and improve coverage even farther, wirelessly or with wires.
- **Multiple 2.5 gigabit Ethernet ports:** Ethernet ports on a mesh system’s satellites let you connect devices such as TVs, streaming boxes, and gaming consoles away from the base unit.
- **Nice-to-have extras:** Fast, reliable Wi-Fi is what matters the most in a mesh system, but more expensive optional features bring other benefits, too. The things we like to see that justify spending more for a mesh system include speedier connections (like 5 or 10 gigabit ports), extra Ethernet ports, and tri-band Wi-Fi 7.
- **Price:** You can buy a mesh system for $80; you can also spend over $2,200. But we don’t consider the cheapest or the fastest to be the best. When considering both features and our test results, we looked for “the best for the most for the least.”

One more thing: Don’t confuse the test results in our guide with the internet speed you’re paying for. For example, the TP-Link Deco BE63 (our upgrade pick) is capable of downloading over 950 megabits per second at close range with no obstructions, but you still can’t get more than about 100 megabits per second from the internet if you have a 100-megabit plan from your ISP.

The Eero app is easy to use. Compatibility and support are solid. All currently available Eero systems are compatible with each other. Because Eero’s parent company is Amazon, recent Echo devices and the Alexa voice assistant are also supported.

It can handle a lot of traffic. In our responsiveness tests, the Eero 7 was competitive with the best mesh systems. Even when the Wi-Fi network was at its most congested, the Eero 7 was able to stream 4K videos, download large files, and keep multiple web browsing sessions lag-free. Other more expensive systems, such as [Asus’s ZenWifi BQ16 Pro](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/64179/205425/4/193683/?merchant=Walmart), slowed down when the network was busy.

**Eero offers cellular internet backup.** Eero is one of the first mesh network makers to offer plug-and-play internet backup in the [Amazon Eero Signal](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/78008/232251/4/243146/?merchant=Amazon), a separate accessory that offers 4G LTE connectivity for $100 or 5G for $200. You keep the Eero Signal plugged in next to the base unit, and it will switch over automatically when your primary internet goes down. The $100 annual Eero Plus subscription includes 10 GB of data, and with Eero Plus 100, you can get up to 100 GB per month for $200 a year. Other manufacturers, like [D-Link](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/68100/212500/4/250894/?merchant=Amazon), offer mesh networks with 5G data modems built in. But the Eero Signal doesn’t require you to subscribe to a separate cellular data plan; the backup service is included in (and administered by) Eero Plus. The Eero Signal could be a viable backup if you have occasionally spotty internet service that can be knocked out by construction in your area or by heavy rain or snow storms. We’ll be testing the Eero Signal soon.

An Eero Plus subscription also includes an option for [internet backup](https://support.eero.com/hc/en-us/articles/8947860842523-What-is-eero-Internet-Backup) that uses your phone. You can connect your phone with the Eero app and use its limited hotspot data, which can be handy, but it might not always be a reliable backup option.

Either internet backup is usable if your primary internet is unavailable (such as in a power failure or if your cable or fiber ISP is down). But you’ll need a separate battery backup, like a [portable power station](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-portable-power-stations/), to keep the Eero 7 running if the power goes out.

### Flaws but not dealbreakers

**The simple design sacrifices ports.** It has only one extra 2.5-gig Ethernet port on the base unit and two on the second router. In contrast, our upgrade pick, the TP-Link Deco BE63, has four 2.5-gig ports on each router.

**The optional subscription is pricey.** Eero charges between $100 and $120 per year for its Eero Plus subscription, which is necessary to access parental controls and anti-malware protection — services that Asus’s ZenWiFi systems include for free. The Eero Plus subscription is entirely optional, however: The Wi-Fi network will continue to work without the parental controls and anti-malware.

**It’s dual-band Wi-Fi 7.** To save on costs and reduce complexity, the Eero 7 eschews the 6 GHz radio that more expensive Wi-Fi 7 and 6E mesh routers have. Eero also omitted that feature to make the Eero 7 available in countries and territories that heavily regulate the 6 GHz radio bands. 6 GHz bands may be faster, but they also have a shorter range than 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands. This ultimately makes the Eero 7 less attractive if you already have Wi-Fi 7–equipped laptops and if you have an internet connection faster than 2.5 Gig. If you’re part of that early-adopter group, our upgrade pick might be a better choice.

**You have to administer the mesh network in the cloud.** Technically, bad actors could [monitor your data](https://www.cnet.com/home/internet/your-router-is-collecting-your-data-privacy-explainer/) via this login, though Amazon and Eero claim [that they safeguard](https://support.eero.com/hc/en-us/articles/207957013-What-type-of-data-does-eero-collect-and-why-) your data and online activities. Mesh networks from Asus, Linksys, and Netgear also use apps for login and setup, but the latter three (and a few others) give you an option to administer each router locally via a web page.

**You have to use the Eero app to control the network. It’s not flexible.** There are very few settings you can actually change in the app, which makes Eero not as flexible as its rivals. Many standalone routers and some of the other mesh systems listed here will let you set parameters like 5 GHz and 2.4 GHz channels, wireless signal strengths, and the ability to create separate network names (SSIDs) for keeping smart-home devices separate from your laptop network.

While it is now [owned by Amazon](https://www.theverge.com/2019/2/11/18220960/amazon-eero-acquisition-announced), the Eero company developed and released its first two generations of mesh networks independently of Amazon. We think that the ease of setup, frequent firmware updates (as needed), ability to add Eero units to expand coverage, ability to manage a network remotely, and company’s proven track record outweigh these concerns.

Senior staff writer Tim Heffernan installed an Eero 6+ to provide a “strong, solid” connection to a back room that our former [Wi-Fi extender](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-wi-fi-extender/) upgrade pick, the Asus RP-AX56, couldn’t reach in their apartment. ### Flaws but not dealbreakers

You can sign up for five gigabit and eight gigabit internet home plans in selected areas of the US, but service at those speeds are expensive and service areas are limited at this time. The 1 gig and 2 gig plans are much more readily available and accessible.

If you want to connect two or more devices to the Deco base unit, or more than three to any other mesh unit, you’ll need an [Ethernet switch](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/blog/what-is-a-network-switch/).

**The Deco BE23 isn’t as simple or easy to use as the Eero 7.** While the Deco’s app and administration website are easy to set up and tweak, the Eero 7’s app is even simpler. For example, Eero’s parental controls are built into its app (and they don’t show up unless you manually set up your children's individual profiles with devices), while the TP-Link Deco BE23 requires a [separate](https://www.tp-link.com/us/support/faq/3781/) [KidShield](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/78026/232279/4/243175/?merchant=Apple%20App%20Store) [app](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.tplink.kidshield&hl=en_US) to lock down and administer your children’s phones. Other features, like Eero Signal and Eero Simple Setup, are simpler fixes for problems such as ISP disconnections and adding additional network nodes, respectively, compared with what TP-Link offers.

## Other mesh networks worth considering

**If you have more technical needs:** We tested a pair of Ubiquiti’s [UniFi Express 7](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/69602/215265/4/216407/?merchant=B%26H) (UX7) mesh-capable routers, and they fared much better than when we tested one alone as part of our recent [standalone router testing](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-wi-fi-router/#what-about-ubiquiti). When we connected our seven laptops to the Express 7 network, each laptop was able to download data, stream simulated 4K video, and browse simulated websites all at once, with nary a hiccup or slowdown.

The Unifi Express 7 (and its related Unifi Dream Router 7) were almost as easy to set up as the TP-Link Deco 7 and Eero 7 picks. They weren’t pre-paired like our top picks. But after you’ve set up the initial Express 7 base unit, powering on a factory-fresh Express 7 unit nearby will automatically notify you in the Unifi app, where you can accept its offer to pair and mesh the new UX7 to the network.

When we tested the [Ubiquiti Unifi Dream Router 7](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/69600/215230/4/216406/?merchant=UniFi) with a single Unifi Express 7 as a mesh access point, it performed well, but not as well as a pair of Unifi Express 7 routers in a mesh network. The Unifi Dream Router 7 and Unifi Express 7 both have similar CPUs, memory, and Wi-Fi radios. So we’d recommend saving some money and setting up a mesh network comprised of Express 7 boxes — unless you need the Unifi Dream Router 7’s extra Ethernet ports, DVR storage, and compatibility with Ubiquiti’s Unifi digital surveillance cameras, door lock controls, and support for business solutions like EV chargers and digital signs.

After its installation, the Ubiquiti Unifi system’s strength is the comprehensive app and administration website, which will let you monitor and tweak the Wi-Fi network to your geeky little heart’s content. Want to turn off individual 2.4 GHz radios on the satellite unit but leave them functioning on the base unit? That’s easy. Need to create a temporary isolated Wi-Fi guest network for the weekend, while your relatives are visiting, but turn it off on Monday? Child’s play. Want to monitor your data usage to avoid going over a data cap? It does that, too. Ubiquiti’s helpful admin website and app will let you know which of your phones, laptops, or gaming consoles are having issues connecting to the Wi-Fi network. So you can make a decision to relocate an Express 7 router or add another one to the mix, or even lock a device to a particular access point so it doesn’t switch to a weaker signal inadvertently. This is the mesh network for networking gearheads and IT pros who want to spend as much (or as little) time troubleshooting a home that has intermittent Wi-Fi issues.

**If you want the best performance possible (for a really high price):** The [Netgear Orbi 870](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/68157/212598/4/208335/?merchant=NETGEAR) is one step below the top-of-the-line Netgear Orbi kit, but it did outperform the [Orbi 970](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/out/link/57771/194197/4/169117/?merchant=NETGEAR), which costs almost twice as much. It was one of the few mesh kits to pass and exceed all of our performance tests with flying colors, no complaints. It was the fastest (or second) on all of our speed and congestion tests. This is rare, since even our top picks will have a forgivable flaw or two on the performance tests.

The Orbi 870 (aka the Orbi 873) is the mesh network we’d recommend for folks with deep pockets who are considering a 5- or 10-gig internet service plan. Its three-piece kit has a 10-gig Ethernet port on the base unit and multiple 2.5-gig ports for your other devices. At $900, it’s too expensive even for upgrade-pick status, and it offers a short one-year warranty. But it is impressive if you have the money.

## How we tested mesh-networking systems

With just a few exceptions, the testing for most Wi-Fi router reviews consists of connecting a single device to Wi-Fi at various distances, trying to get the biggest throughput number possible, and declaring the router with the biggest number and the best range the winner, at least in terms of raw performance. The problem with this method is that it assumes that a big number for one connected device divides evenly into bigger numbers for all devices. This is usually true for wired networking, but it doesn’t work well for Wi-Fi.

Instead of testing for the maximum throughput from a single laptop, we used seven, spaced around our test home, in order to simulate the real-world activity of a busy home network. The test home measures over 3,000 square feet, with three floors of living space and a garage with cinder block interior walls.

### Placement of network devices in our test home

![A floor plan showing a testing layout.](https://cdn.thewirecutter.com/wp-content/media/2025/12/BEST-WI-FI-MESH-BEST-WI-FI-MESH-NETWORKING-SYSTEMS-Floorplan-2048-706x1024.png)

These illustrations show where we placed the pieces of each mesh kit in our real-world test environment. NYT Wirecutter

We verified that the core features of a mesh network were enabled, namely using a single network name (or SSID) to allow roaming for both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz channels.

We placed the main router or node in the living room, in the center of our testing space, and connected it to our [cable modem](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-cable-modem/) via Ethernet. We connected a desktop PC via Ethernet to the main router as our web server and used the open-source [Netburn test](https://github.com/jimsalterjrs/network-testing) to check each router’s speed and responsiveness. We placed the second node in the attic on the third floor of the home, and the third mesh node (if we used one) was positioned in the primary bedroom on the first floor, with one interior wall between it and the base router.

The seven laptops (see the diagram above) were placed throughout the home, on all three floors and in the garage situated close to the cinder block foundation. If the mesh system supported Wi-Fi 6E or 7, we placed a Wi-Fi 7 laptop in the kitchen.

During testing, the seven laptops, a test web server, our wired controller laptop, and an Apple iPhone running the router app (if needed) were the only devices connected to the test network. We let the surrounding Wi-Fi networks and wireless devices like Google Home speakers do their usual noisy things, just as they probably do in your home. The neighbors and our home network also kept their Wi-Fi networks going, which left somewhere in the vicinity of a half dozen to a dozen network names visible at any given time.

Our seven laptops ran the following tests:

- **Browsers:** Three laptops simulated real-human web browsing by loading a “web page” once every 20 seconds. Each “web page” consisted of 16 separate 128 KB files, all requested simultaneously. This is the most important test—it accurately represents real web browsing—and it usually fails before any of the other tests do.
- **Downloader:** One or two laptops downloaded very large files. We wanted to see an overall throughput of 100 Mbps or better, to simulate the experience of downloading a large game file or an update. This test is a big challenge for the rest of the network—if these laptops get all of the available airtime, the other tests suffer.
- **Video streamers:** Two laptops each simulated a 4K video streaming session. They tried to download data at up to 30 Mbps, but we were satisfied if they could average 25 Mbps or better, which is [what Disney+ recommends for 4K UHD](https://help.disneyplus.com/csp?id=csp_article_content&sys_kb_id=74de48a91b0d8010a24eb8c11a4bcbe2).

These tests simultaneously evaluated range, throughput, and the router’s ability to multitask. We ran all these tests at the same time for a full five minutes to simulate a realistic extra-busy time on a home network. Although your network probably isn’t always that busy, those busy times are when you’re most likely to experience slowdowns. We ran each test six times and averaged the results.

We also tested raw speed in terms of throughput at the farthest spot in the attic/sunroom, at a closer spot about 15 feet away with no obstructions, an unobstructed spot in the kitchen 25 feet away (when testing Wi-Fi 6E or 7), and in a bedroom on the second floor with several interior walls in between the laptop and the main router.

Testing mesh systems this way—with a mix of easy and difficult spots to reach—ensures that we find the ones that work best throughout your house, rather than just looking good in the easy spots.

In addition to testing for raw throughput and the quality of web browsing, we made sure roaming worked well on our picks by checking each router’s interface (if present) to confirm that all the laptops weren’t bunched on a single node, router, or satellite.

## An overview of the test results

We believe that mesh’s purpose isn’t to make Wi-Fi fast somewhere—it’s to make Wi-Fi fast everywhere.

So we tested throughput in three spots in our test space, with increasing degrees of difficulty: a spot in the living room, with a clear 15-foot run to the mesh base unit; in the attic, where the test laptop would be best situated to connect to the satellite node there; and a spot in one of the home’s bedrooms, where the laptop could choose to connect to a satellite or through several walls to the main router.

### Wi-Fi 7 speed-test results (Mbps)

![A chart showing speed test results.](https://cdn.thewirecutter.com/wp-content/media/2025/12/BEST-WI-FI-MESH-BEST-WI-FI-MESH-NETWORKING-SYSTEMS-SpeedTest-2048px-1024x814.png)

Adding a Wi-Fi 7 connection can give you enviable speeds provided you have a strong signal and use a Wi-Fi 7 laptop or phone. NYT Wirecutter

Speed (aka throughput) is the statistic that most mesh system manufacturers promote. The better-performing mesh systems, including our upgrade pick, the tri-band TP-Link Deco BE63, were able to handle each situation adroitly and provided over 975 Mbps without obstructions, over 815 Mbps at close range, and 77 Mbps to 255 Mbps in the other situations. Our top pick, the Eero 7, was a little slower, but still competitive, providing 806 Mbps close by and 56 Mbps to 560 Mbps otherwise.

This efficient relay system is the hallmark of a good mesh network—you can see that in the “through one wall” and “through multiple walls” results. Good tri-band systems like the Deco BE63 utilized their speedy 5 GHz and 6 GHz radios to relay network signals efficiently to the base station.

That speed-test graph is worth looking at to get an idea of your best-case performance when you’re the only one on the network, but those test results don’t tell the most important story. To do that, we needed multiple laptops, simulating a busy real-world network, [as we described](#how-we-tested-mesh-networking-kits) above. A brief recap: We had two laptops simulating 4K video screens, one or two downloading large files, and three laptops browsing the web.

The web browsing test is both the most realistic representation of your experience in using your Wi-Fi and the test that almost always fails before any other test does.

Check out this graph, which gives you a sense of how long you might have to wait around for things to load. Bad numbers indicate a poor browsing experience, and, as we’ve said, slow internet sucks.

### Browsing the web on a busy network

![A chart of how effective the different routers were at different levels of network activity.](https://cdn.thewirecutter.com/wp-content/media/2025/12/BEST-WI-FI-MESH-BEST-WI-FI-MESH-NETWORKING-SYSTEMS-BusyNetwork-2048px.png?width=320&quality=60&crop=2049:1781&auto=webp)

A chart of how effective the different routers were at different levels of network activity.

For web browsing, latency—how long it takes between a request and a response—is more important than raw throughput. High latency, or lag, can make an otherwise speedy connection seem to drag, especially when the network is busy. By looking at slowdowns while all six laptops were working simultaneously, we got a good measure of how well the network handles congestion.

The chart shows what percentage of the time our test networks delivered a satisfactorily fast browsing experience. While some of the other networks were dragged down when the Wi-Fi was congested (the yellow, orange, and red areas), our top performers easily handled the vast majority of these tests. The Eero 7, TP-Link Deco BE63, and Deco BE23 all performed admirably on a crowded network, which largely contributed to their finishing as our top, upgrade, and budget picks, respectively.

If a mesh network rockets above 2,000 milliseconds, for example, that means a high percentage of frustratingly slow page loads. This is a tough test, but we think a good network should respond reliably all the time.

We wanted to test an inexpensive mesh system like the TP-Link Deco BE23 and compare it with the other mesh networks here. The Deco BE23 also showed excellent performance on our tests overall, remaining competitive with much more expensive systems like the Netgear Orbi 373, Acer Predator T7 Mesh, and Motorola MNQ1525. That rock steady connectivity on a busy network earned the Deco BE23 a nod as our budget pick.

## The FCC’s foreign-made router ban

On March 23, 2026, the FCC [announced](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/foreign-made-wi-fi-routers-ban/) that it will not approve certification for new routers and mesh networks designed and assembled in countries outside the US, unless the manufacturer applies for and receives a conditional approval certification for each router from the Department of Defense and the Department of Homeland Security.

As of April 2026, [Eero](https://blog.eero.com/u-s-government-recognizes-eero-as-a-trusted-and-secure-provider-of-routers/) and [Netgear](https://www.netgear.com/letter-from-the-ceo-fcc-conditional-approval/) are the only router manufacturers to receive [conditional approval](https://www.fcc.gov/supplychain/coveredlist#conditional-approvals) for their consumer networking products, including routers, mesh networks, cable gateways, and cable modems. This [exemption](https://www.fcc.gov/supplychain/coveredlist#conditional-approvals) lasts until October 1, 2027, for Netgear and October 31, 2027, for Eero. It remains unclear if Eero and Netgear will have to apply for an extension after October 2027, as the FCC is still hammering out the details and procedures that router manufacturers will have to follow.

The reasons cited for the ban include the Volt, Flax, and Salt Typhoon cyberattacks in which Wi-Fi router security vulnerabilities have been exploited. Router manufacturers update router firmware to protect against these vulnerabilities once they are identified. You can keep your current and future mesh network safe by updating their firmware regularly, using the auto-update feature that is enabled by default in every mesh network we’ve reviewed in the past few years. Router makers will be able to issue [software updates](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2026/05/fcc-slightly-relaxes-foreign-router-ban-allows-software-updates-until-2029/) for existing routers until January 1, 2029.

Consumer-grade mesh networks and their components are generally made in Southeast Asian countries, including Indonesia, Taiwan, Thailand, and Vietnam. In the long term, manufacturers could possibly move design functions and manufacturing to the US.

This process will take time. Industry analyst Mike Feibus of FeibusTech told us: “US consumers will still need to buy routers. Which means there’s still a lot to sort out. Like what companies’ products get a pass via the Conditional Approval list? And how does the government come to define foreign made? This is far from settled."

For now, we will continue to monitor the situation. All of the mesh networks reviewed and tested in our guides have previously been approved by the FCC, and will continue to be available as long as the manufacturers restock them. We stand by our picks.
